# Chapter 11: The Judge

The Nevada desert shimmered with heat, but Jon barely noticed. His enhanced metabolism regulated his temperature effortlessly as he walked toward the facility, toward the sound of Agent Zero's roar.

The itch was a living thing now, coiled in his chest like a serpent, hungry and patient. It knew what was coming. A real fight. A worthy opponent. The kind of violence that would bring a quench so powerful it would silence everything for hours, maybe days.

Jon's hands weren't shaking anymore. They were steady. Calm. Ready.

*This is what you were made for,* the cold voice whispered. *Not saving people. Killing them. Efficiently. Perfectly. Beautifully.*

Jon didn't argue with the voice anymore. There was no point.

He crested a ridge and saw the facility spread below him—a collection of industrial buildings that looked innocuous from this distance. But Jon's enhanced vision could see the details: guard towers with automated weapons, patrol patterns, the reinforced entrance he'd destroyed being repaired.

And in the open ground between the ridge and the facility, standing perfectly still like a statue carved from nightmares, was Agent Zero.

Matteo "Ghost" Rask had deteriorated even further since the Nexus fight. The Creep-1 lattice had continued its parasitic spread. His entire left side was covered in jagged graphene armor that looked like it had grown from his flesh rather than been added to it. His face was barely recognizable as human—one eye clouded and dead, the other glowing with that sickly green bioluminescence.

But the worst part was the sounds he made. Not roars of rage. Not battle cries.

Whimpering. Like an animal caught in a trap, suffering beyond endurance.

Jon descended the ridge slowly, deliberately. He didn't try to hide. Didn't try to maneuver for tactical advantage.

This wasn't that kind of fight.

When he was a hundred meters away, Zero's head snapped toward him. That one good eye focused with perfect, terrible clarity.

"Six..." The voice was wet, broken, barely human. "Came... back..."

"Yeah," Jon said. "I came back."

"Why?"

"To finish what I started. To end Kross. To make sure no one else becomes what we are."

Zero made a sound that might have been a laugh or a sob. "Too... late... for me..."

"I know."

"Then... why?"

Jon stopped fifty meters away. Close enough to talk. Far enough to react if Zero charged.

"Because you were a soldier, Matteo. EU Defense Force. You served with honor. You didn't deserve what Kross did to you. And you don't deserve to spend whatever time you have left as his weapon."

Zero's massive frame shook. The graphene armor creaked and groaned, the sound of metal under impossible stress.

"Can't... stop it..." Zero's voice was agonized. "Lattice... spreading... taking over... soon... nothing left... just pain... just weapon..."

"I can stop it," Jon said quietly. "If you want me to."

Zero went perfectly still. Even the whimpering stopped.

"How?"

"There's a neural cluster at the base of your skull. Where the Creep-1 interfaces with your brain stem. If I hit it precisely, with enough force, it'll shut down the lattice. Instantly. Permanently."

"And... me?"

"You'll die. But it'll be fast. Painless. You won't suffer anymore."

The silence stretched between them. In the distance, Jon could hear the facility's alarms, the movement of guards, the mechanical hum of defensive systems powering up.

Kross knew they were here. He was watching, probably. Waiting to see what his two weapons would do to each other.

"You... would do that?" Zero asked. "Mercy... kill?"

"If you ask me to. If that's what you want."

"Why... not just... fight? Winner... takes all?"

Jon thought about that. About how easy it would be to frame this as combat, as self-defense. To tell himself he had no choice, that Zero attacked him, that the killing was justified.

But he was done lying to himself.

"Because you're not my enemy, Matteo. You're a victim. Just like I am. And if our positions were reversed, if I was the one trapped in agony, begging for release—I'd want someone to give me the mercy of an end."

Zero's eye closed. When it opened again, there was something different in it. Something that looked almost like peace.

"Jon... Rivian..." Zero said, speaking clearly for the first time. The man beneath the monster, surfacing one last time. "That's... your name? Your... real name?"

"Yes."

"Thank you... Jon Rivian... for seeing me... not just... the weapon..."

Zero straightened. The whimpering stopped. The trembling stopped. He stood tall, or as tall as his corrupted frame would allow, and for just a moment he looked like what he must have been before Kross destroyed him—a soldier. Proud. Brave. Ready.

"I'm ready," Matteo Rask said. "End it."

---

Jon crossed the fifty meters in three seconds.

The itch was screaming—not in anticipation of violence, but in confusion. This wasn't right. This wasn't a fight. This wasn't what it wanted.

But Jon wasn't listening to the itch anymore.

He came in low and fast. Zero didn't defend himself, didn't raise his massive arms to block. He just stood there, head tilted back, exposing the neural cluster.

Jon's hand came up, fingers rigid, reinforced by the graphene lattice until they were harder than steel.

The strike was perfect. Precise. The neural cluster shattered.

Zero's body went rigid. The green glow in his eye flickered once, twice, then went dark. The graphene armor began to dull, the corrupted lattice dying as its connection to Zero's nervous system severed.

Matteo Rask collapsed forward, and Jon caught him. All nine feet and 400 pounds of corrupted super-soldier, and Jon held him as gently as a child.

"Thank... you..." Matteo whispered, his voice clear now, free of pain for the first time in eighteen months.

Then he was gone.

Jon lowered the body to the desert floor. Matteo's face, no longer twisted in agony, looked almost peaceful. The graphene armor was already beginning to crumble, breaking down without the lattice to maintain it.

Jon stood over the body of the man who'd been Agent Zero, and waited for the quench.

It didn't come.

The itch was silent, yes. But there was no euphoria. No flood of satisfaction. Just... emptiness.

He'd killed someone. Crossed the line he'd sworn he wouldn't cross.

*It was mercy,* he told himself. *It was the right thing to do.*

But mercy-kill or murder, the result was the same. Matteo Rask was dead by Jon's hand.

And somewhere deep inside, beneath the rationalizations and justifications, Jon Rivian understood that he'd just become exactly what he'd been fighting against.

A killer.

---

The facility's main entrance was fully repaired now, the blast doors Jon had destroyed replaced with new ones. Guards stood at attention. Automated turrets tracked his approach.

And standing in front of it all, watching through the security cameras, was Dr. Elias Kross.

Jon walked toward the entrance like a man approaching a gallows. His own, or someone else's, he couldn't quite tell.

When he was a hundred meters from the entrance, a loudspeaker crackled to life.

"Agent Six." Kross's voice, amplified and distorted. "You killed Agent Zero. Our most powerful asset. I must say, I'm impressed."

Jon didn't respond. He just kept walking.

"But you hesitated. You gave him a mercy kill. That's not efficiency, Agent Six. That's sentimentality. Weakness." A pause. "The man you used to be is still in there, isn't he? Still fighting. Still clinging to the idea that you're a hero."

Jon stopped fifty meters from the entrance.

"I'm not a hero," he said, his voice carrying across the desert silence. "And I'm not your weapon anymore. I'm something else. Something you created but can't control."

"Oh, I don't need to control you, Agent Six. You're doing exactly what I designed you to do. You're hunting. You're killing. You're becoming the perfect predator." Kross's voice held a note of satisfaction. "The only question is whether you'll direct that predation at worthy targets, or whether you'll become a mindless killer. And that decision, I'm afraid, is yours alone."

"Open the doors," Jon said.

"Why would I do that?"

"Because I'm coming in either way. And if you make me tear through your defenses, a lot of your people are going to get hurt. Open the doors, let me walk in, and I promise—this is between you and me. No one else has to die."

Silence.

Then, slowly, the blast doors began to open.

---

The facility's main atrium was empty except for Dr. Elias Kross, standing in the center like a man awaiting judgment.

He wasn't armed. Wasn't wearing body armor. Just his usual tailored suit, looking calm and composed as Jon entered.

"You kept your word," Kross said. "No collateral damage. I appreciate that."

"Where is everyone?" Jon asked.

"I evacuated non-essential personnel. The guards, the scientists, the support staff—they're all in the secure bunkers on Level Four. It's just you and me now." Kross gestured at the empty atrium. "I thought we should have this conversation in private."

Jon's enhanced senses swept the space. No hidden guards. No turrets. No traps that he could detect.

Just Kross.

"You're not afraid," Jon observed.

"Should I be? You just gave Agent Zero a mercy kill. You could have left him to suffer, could have let Matteo Rask die slowly and in agony. But you didn't. You showed compassion." Kross smiled. "You're still trying to be the hero, Agent Six. And heroes don't murder unarmed civilians."

"You're not a civilian. You're a war criminal."

"I'm a scientist who made difficult choices in service of his country. There's a difference."

Jon crossed the atrium slowly, closing the distance. Kross didn't back away. Didn't flinch.

"You murdered me," Jon said. "Sabotaged my HALO rig. Shattered my body. Then rebuilt me into a weapon without my consent."

"I saved your life. You were clinically dead. I brought you back."

"As a puppet."

"As a tool. A very expensive, very powerful tool." Kross's expression hardened. "Jon Rivian died in that HALO jump. I don't deny that. But Agent Six was born. And Agent Six has the potential to save thousands of lives, to prevent wars, to protect American interests in a world that's becoming more dangerous every day."

"By becoming a remote-controlled assassin?"

"By becoming a precision instrument. The control protocol existed for operational security. To prevent exactly what you've become—a rogue weapon with no guidance, no purpose, no handler."

Jon was close now. Ten feet away. Close enough to smell Kross's cologne, see the perfectly tied knot of his tie.

"I met five people before you," Jon said quietly. "Five subjects who came before me. Four of them died in agony. The fifth became Agent Zero—a nightmare you kept in cryogenic suspension because even you couldn't control what you'd created."

"Regrettable losses," Kross said. "But necessary for progress. Science requires sacrifice."

"They weren't yours to sacrifice."

"They were soldiers. Volunteers. They understood the risks."

"Did they understand you'd turn them into addicts? That you'd program them to crave violence? That you'd build a biological leash into their very cells?" Jon's voice was cold, flat. "Did you tell them that, Dr. Kross? Or did you lie to them the way you lied to me?"

For the first time, Kross's composure cracked. Just slightly. A flicker of something in his eyes. Not guilt. Not remorse.

Pride.

"I created gods," Kross said. "I took broken, damaged humans and elevated them. You're faster than any Olympic athlete. Stronger than any powerlifter. Smarter than any savant. You're everything humanity could become if we just had the courage to evolve."

"At what cost?"

"Any cost. All costs." Kross's voice rose, passionate now, and Jon saw something flicker across his face—pain, briefly unguarded. "Don't you understand, Agent Six? The Pan-Asia European War showed us the future. Humans are obsolete. Drones, AI, autonomous weapons—they're faster, cheaper, more reliable than flesh and blood. The only way humanity survives the next century is if we become something more. Something better. Something like you."

He took a breath, and for a moment his composure slipped. "My daughter understood that. Sophie. She had osteosarcoma—bone cancer. Stage four. I watched her waste away for eighteen months while the best doctors in the world told me there was nothing they could do. But the Creep technology... if I'd had just two more years, if I'd finished the cellular regeneration protocols, I could have saved her."

Jon's eyes narrowed. "So this is about your daughter?"

"This is about everyone's daughter!" Kross's voice cracked. "Every parent who's watched their child die from a disease we should be able to cure. Every soldier who comes home in pieces because their fragile human body couldn't withstand modern warfare. We have the technology to fix it, Agent Six. The Creep lattice could eliminate cancer, regenerate damaged organs, prevent paralysis. But everyone's too afraid of the military applications to let us help people."

"So you decided to build weapons first."

"I decided to prove it works! If the military sees what the Creep technology can do—make soldiers unkillable, unstoppable—then the funding comes. And once we have funding, once we perfect the integration, we can use it for everything else. For healing. For saving lives."

"You mean once your backers see a return on their investment."

Kross's expression shifted—just for a moment, surprise flickering across his face. "What do you know about my backers?"

"I know DARPA shut down your original program. I know the DOD wouldn't touch Creep-6 after what happened to the first five subjects. So someone else funded this facility. Someone else bankrolled MOSAIK. Someone with deep enough pockets to build a black site in Nevada and keep it off the books." Jon stepped closer. "Who are they, Dr. Kross? Who do you really work for?"

Kross's jaw tightened. "You don't understand the scope of what we're doing."

"Then explain it to me."

"There are people—powerful people, across multiple governments and corporations—who understand that humanity is at an extinction crossroads. The Pan-Asia European War was just the beginning. Climate collapse. Resource wars. AI proliferation. We're facing threats that nation-states can't handle alone." Kross's voice took on an almost religious fervor. "The Consortium sees what others don't: that the only way to survive the next century is to transcend our limitations. To become more than human."

"The Consortium," Jon repeated. "That's what they call themselves?"

"It's not an official name. It's a network. Philanthropists. Defense contractors. Intelligence agencies. Tech billionaires. People who think in decades and centuries, not election cycles." Kross met his eyes. "MOSAIK isn't just my project, Agent Six. It's the first step in a much larger program. Creep-6 is just the beginning."

"You're rationalizing," Jon said. "You turned people into killing machines and told yourself it was for the greater good."

"As if you're so different." Kross's eyes gleamed, and he pulled back his left sleeve.

Jon's breath caught.

Kross's forearm was covered in hexagonal patterns beneath the skin. Faint, much less pronounced than Jon's, but unmistakable.

Graphene lattice.

"You think I'd ask others to accept something I wouldn't try myself?" Kross said quietly. "This is Creep-2. The second iteration, designed for medical applications before we pivoted to military enhancement. I've been testing it for eight years, since before Sophie died. It's kept me alive longer than I should be—I have stage three pancreatic cancer, Agent Six. Should have killed me five years ago. But the lattice is fighting it. Slowly losing, but fighting."

He rolled his sleeve back down. "Creep-1 was Rask—too aggressive, parasitic integration that consumed the host. I learned from that failure. Creep-2 is gentler, focused on cellular regeneration rather than enhancement. But it's imperfect—unstable, degrades over time. Creep-3 through 5 were attempts to find the balance. Different subjects, different approaches. Some focused on cognition. Others on healing. One was designed for stealth operations—adaptive camouflage at the cellular level."

Jon's eyes narrowed. "What happened to them?"

"Creep-3 died when the neural enhancement triggered cascading seizures. Creep-4 achieved stable integration but the subject developed psychological fractures—multiple personalities as the lattice created alternate neural pathways. Creep-5..." Kross paused. "Creep-5 was the closest we got before you. Stable. Powerful. But the subject couldn't handle the itch. Went rogue, had to be terminated by a joint CIA-JSOC task force in São Paulo."

"And then you built me."

"Creep-6. The perfect synthesis. We took everything that worked—the stability of 2, the enhancement of 3, the behavioral conditioning from 4, the control protocols developed after 5's failure. You're the masterwork, Agent Six. Everything that came before was just practice."

Jon felt sick. "You're talking about human beings like they're prototypes."

"They were prototypes. Necessary steps in the development process." Kross met his eyes without shame. "And there will be more after you. Creep-7 is already in early development. The Consortium has approved funding for a full production run—fifty units minimum. Your success proved the concept works. Now we scale up."

"Something like a monster."

"What's the difference between a monster and a god? Perspective. Power. Purpose." Kross stepped closer, his eyes burning with fervor—and Jon could see it now, the slight tremor in his hands, the way he moved like a man fighting pain. "You think I'm a villain because I made you crave violence? I gave you focus. Direction. The itch and the quench are gifts, Agent Six. They make you perfect at what you do. They remove hesitation, fear, doubt—all the weaknesses that plague normal humans."

"They make me an addict."

"They make you efficient!" Kross slammed his hand on a nearby table. "Yes, you need to hunt. Yes, you need to kill. But that's not a bug, it's a feature! You're a weapon, Agent Six. Weapons don't second-guess. They don't hesitate. They execute their function perfectly, every time."

Jon looked at Kross—this brilliant, twisted man who'd built a god and couldn't understand why his creation hated him for it.

"I'm not going to kill you," Jon said.

Kross blinked. "What?"

"You heard me. I'm not going to kill you. Killing you would be too easy. Too quick. You'd become a martyr, a genius cut down by his own creation. People would study you, write papers about you, maybe even continue your work."

"Then what—"

Jon moved.

Faster than Kross could track, faster than any normal human could react, Jon's hand shot out and grabbed Kross by the throat. Not to strangle. Just to hold.

"I'm going to do something worse," Jon said quietly. "I'm going to make you live."

---

Jon dragged Kross through the facility, down to Level Two, to the medical wing where he'd first woken up after his rebirth. Kross struggled, tried to shout, but Jon's grip was iron.

The medical bay was exactly as Jon remembered—emergence tanks, surgical suites, equipment that cost more than most people would earn in a lifetime.

Jon threw Kross into one of the surgical chairs, the kind designed for delicate neural procedures. Built-in restraints activated automatically, locking Kross in place.

"What are you doing?" Kross demanded, fear creeping into his voice for the first time.

"Giving you what you deserve."

Jon pulled up a holographic medical interface, his quantum-enhanced brain processing the information instantly. The medical systems here were designed for Creep-6 integration procedures, but they could be repurposed.

He found what he was looking for: a precision neural scalpel, capable of performing surgery at the microscopic level.

"You're going to lobotomize me," Kross said, his voice tight.

"No. Lobotomy destroys the brain. I'm going to do something much more precise." Jon activated the neural scalpel, began calibrating it. "You know what your mistake was, Dr. Kross? You gave me a quantum-level brain. Perfect processing power. Perfect memory. Perfect understanding of complex systems."

Jon pulled up a three-dimensional model of Kross's brain, scanned in real-time by the medical equipment.

"Including," Jon continued, "a perfect understanding of human neurology."

Kross went pale. "You wouldn't."

"I would." Jon's voice was cold, clinical. "I'm going to sever the connection between your motor cortex and your brain stem. Not destroy it. Just disconnect it. You'll still be able to think, feel, see, hear, smell, taste. You'll still be you. But you won't be able to move. Not your arms. Not your legs. Not even your eyelids."

"That's... that's monstrous."

"Is it?" Jon positioned the neural scalpel. "Let me tell you what's monstrous. You wanted to turn me into a perfect weapon—optimized for efficiency, programmed to choose the solution with the highest success rate, stripped of human hesitation and doubt. You wanted me to think like Aegis-Com, reducing human lives to probability matrices and acceptable losses."

He met Kross's terrified eyes.

"Killing you would be optimal. Quick, efficient, permanent. A 100% success rate in eliminating the threat you represent. That's what the lattice wants me to do. That's what your programming tells me to do."

Jon shook his head. "But I'm not a machine. I'm not Aegis-Com. I'm a man who chooses messy, imperfect, human justice over algorithmic perfection. And that means you get to live with what you've done. You turned me into a weapon and took away my choice, my agency, my control over my own body. I'm just returning the favor."

"Please—"

"You'll be conscious. Aware. Trapped in a body that won't obey you, no matter how much you scream inside your own head." Jon's voice was cold. "You'll spend the rest of your life as a prisoner in your own flesh. Unable to communicate. Unable to move. Unable to die. Just... existing. Thinking. Remembering what you did. Understanding exactly why this happened to you."

"This is torture! This is—"

"Justice," Jon finished. "Imperfect, inefficient, human justice. You wanted to create gods who optimized for perfect efficiency. Congratulations, Dr. Kross. You succeeded. But your god chose human vengeance over algorithmic optimization. And now he's judging you."

The neural scalpel hummed to life.

Kross screamed.

Jon worked with perfect precision, his quantum brain guiding his hands. The procedure took seven minutes. Seven minutes of the most delicate, controlled violence Jon had ever performed.

When it was done, Kross sat in the chair, his eyes wide with terror, his mouth open in a soundless scream. But his body was perfectly still. Perfectly paralyzed.

Still breathing. Still alive. Still conscious.

Just... helpless.

Jon deactivated the neural scalpel, set it aside.

"Can you hear me?" Jon asked.

Kross's eyes tracked toward him. The only part of his body he could still move.

"Blink once for yes."

A slow, deliberate blink.

"Good. Then listen carefully. I'm going to leave you here. Eventually, someone will find you. They'll run tests, realize what I did, and put you in a care facility somewhere. You'll spend the rest of your life being fed through a tube, unable to speak, unable to move, unable to tell anyone what really happened. They'll think you had a stroke. A brain aneurysm. They'll never know the truth."

Jon leaned in close.

"But you'll know. Every second of every day for the rest of your life, you'll know that this is what you deserve. This is the price for playing god."

Kross's eyes filled with tears. They ran down his cheeks, the only movement his body could still manage.

Jon turned and walked away, leaving Dr. Elias Kross—genius, visionary, war criminal—trapped in his own flesh.

The itch was silent.

The quench never came.

Jon had done something worse than killing. Something darker. Something that should have horrified him.

Instead, he felt... satisfied.

Not the chemical euphoria of the quench. Something deeper. Colder. More permanent.

He'd passed judgment. Delivered punishment. Become executioner and jury in one.

*I'm a monster,* Jon thought as he walked through the facility's corridors, heading toward the data archives.

*Yes,* the cold voice agreed. *But you're the right kind of monster. A judge. A weapon aimed at those who deserve it.*

Jon didn't argue.

---

The facility's data archives were on Level Three, next to the Nexus servers. Jon spent an hour there, his quantum brain interfacing with the systems, downloading everything.

Every experiment. Every subject. Every crime.

The data files on Subjects One through Five—their recruitment, their integration, their suffering, their deaths.

The funding trail showing which agencies, which politicians, which corporations had bankrolled Project MOSAIK.

The communications showing how Kross had suppressed Dr. Rostova's objections, had forced Mira Koval out of the project, had covered up the failures.

All of it. Every piece of evidence needed to destroy MOSAIK permanently.

Jon uploaded it all to a secure server Rostova had set up, programmed to release automatically to every major news organization and intelligence agency on Earth if he didn't check in within 72 hours.

Then he set charges throughout the facility. Not enough to hurt anyone in the bunkers—he'd meant what he said about no collateral damage—but enough to destroy the equipment, the research, the ability to ever rebuild Project MOSAIK.

He gave the trapped personnel thirty minutes to evacuate. Watched on the security cameras as they fled the facility, carrying Kross's paralyzed body with them.

Then he detonated the charges.

The facility collapsed in on itself—a controlled demolition that turned $60 billion of research into rubble and dust.

Jon watched from a ridge a mile away as the Nevada desert swallowed MOSAIK whole.

---

He found Mira at the original rendezvous point, exactly where he'd left her. She looked exhausted, her eyes red from crying or lack of sleep or both.

When she saw him walking out of the darkness, alive and whole, she ran to him.

"You're okay," she breathed. "Oh God, you're okay."

"I'm okay," Jon confirmed.

"Did you...?"

"Kross is alive. Paralyzed but alive. He'll spend the rest of his life trapped in his own body. MOSAIK is destroyed. The data is uploaded to Rostova's server. It's over."

Mira pulled back, studying his face. "And Agent Zero?"

"Dead. I gave him what he asked for. Peace."

"So you killed him."

"Yes."

Mira was silent for a long moment. "How do you feel?"

Jon thought about that. About the emptiness where the quench should have been. About the cold satisfaction he'd felt delivering his judgment on Kross. About the fact that he'd crossed every line he'd sworn he wouldn't cross, and he didn't feel guilty.

"I feel like a stranger," he said honestly. "Like the man I was died in that HALO jump, and what came back is wearing his face but isn't him anymore."

"Are you still Jon?"

"I don't know. Maybe. Parts of him, anyway. The parts Kross couldn't destroy."

Mira took his hand. It was warm, human, a reminder that not everything about him had changed.

"What happens now?" she asked.

"Now you go back to Prague. Rostova will help you disappear, start over. Live the life you should have had before Kross tried to kill you."

"And you?"

Jon looked out at the desert, at the endless expanse of nothing that stretched to the horizon.

"I can't stay here. Can't go back to who I was. The itch is still there, Mira. It'll always be there. And eventually, it's going to push me to do things I can't justify, can't rationalize, can't frame as heroic."

"So what will you do?"

"I'll aim it. Like Rostova said. Find targets that deserve it. People like Kross—the ones who fund projects like MOSAIK, who turn soldiers into weapons, who prey on the vulnerable." He met her eyes. "I'll become what I need to be. A hunter. A judge. A weapon aimed at the people the law can't touch."

"That's a dark road, Jon."

"I know."

"You might lose yourself completely."

"I know that too."

Mira squeezed his hand one last time, then let go.

"Then I hope you find your way back," she said quietly. "Because somewhere in there, beneath all the lattice and the programming and the itch, the man who saved me in Amsterdam is still fighting. And he's worth saving."

She kissed him on the cheek—a gesture of goodbye and hope and mourning all at once—then walked to the SUV.

"Mira," Jon called after her.

She turned.

"Thank you. For seeing me as human when I couldn't see it myself."

She smiled sadly. "Don't thank me yet. Just... try to stay human, Jon. Even if it's hard. Even if the itch tells you otherwise. Try."

Then she drove away, disappearing into the pre-dawn darkness.

Jon stood alone in the Nevada desert, the ruins of MOSAIK smoldering behind him, the body of Agent Zero cooling in the sand, and the memory of Dr. Kross's paralyzed, terrified eyes burned into his mind.

He'd won. Destroyed the project. Freed himself from Kross's control.

But the itch was already starting to rebuild, whispering about new hunts, new targets, new justifications.

And Jon realized with perfect, terrible clarity that the fight wasn't over.

It was just beginning.

Because the war he'd thought was against Kross had always been against something else.

Against himself.

Against the monster wearing his face.

And monsters, Jon was learning, didn't just fight battles.

They fed.
